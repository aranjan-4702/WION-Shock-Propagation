# Introduction and Research Question

## Problem statement
Global production is organized as a network of interdependent sectors. When a supplier is hit by a shock, the effect does not stay local: it moves through intermediate input links and can reach distant sectors and countries. The central question of this thesis is how large those propagated losses become, and how much they are reduced when the network has meaningful absorption capacity.

## Research question
How do supply shocks propagate through the world input-output network, and to what extent does structural and institutional damping reduce those losses?

## Motivation
Traditional aggregate views treat shocks as if they mostly wash out. Network-based production models show that this is not generally true when the economy contains concentrated supplier links, hub sectors, and cross-border dependency. That is the setting studied here.

## Core contribution
This thesis extends a standard input-output propagation model with a damping mechanism that represents supplier substitution and institutional capacity. The result is not just a baseline shock path, but a comparison between undamped and damped propagation under the same shock scenario.

## Key thesis claim
The main finding is that the network is not uniformly fragile. Losses are concentrated in specific sectors and countries, and damping reduces those losses most where the network has substitutability and institutional capacity.
