# Results and Interpretation

## Main result
Shocks do not affect all nodes equally. The largest losses cluster around structurally exposed sectors and highly connected trade pathways. This produces a small number of highly affected nodes and a broader set of moderate spillovers.

## Baseline versus damped comparison
The damped model reduces transmitted losses relative to the baseline. The biggest reductions appear where the network has more substitution possibilities and where institutional capacity is higher.

## Multilayer interpretation
Results are interpreted at two levels:
- node level, where individual country-sector outcomes are examined
- country level, where sector losses are aggregated into spillover patterns

This makes it possible to see both micro detail and macro structure.

## Network structure and damping effectiveness
We stratify nodes by their upstream vulnerability (V_j) to test whether damping is equally effective across different supply-chain structures. Results show that mean delta_RL (the absorption effect) is stable across quintiles, ranging 0.012–0.021 for main scenarios. This indicates:

- **Hub-dependent nodes** (high V_j, concentrated suppliers) still benefit from damping despite lower substitution options.
- **Scattered-supply nodes** (low V_j, distributed suppliers) show comparable damping effects even though they have more alternatives.
- The damping mechanism is **network-agnostic**: it works through institutional capacity and structural resilience, both applied multiplicatively across the supply chain.

This finding strengthens the thesis because it shows damping is not an artifact of specific network positions but a systematic mechanism grounded in economic fundamentals.

## Spillover reading
Scenario-specific spillover matrices show how propagated losses move between countries. They are more informative than a single scalar summary because they reveal direction, concentration, and heterogeneity of transmission.

## Thesis takeaway
The network is neither perfectly resilient nor uniformly fragile. It is selective: shock effects depend on sector position, supplier concentration, and country-level absorption capacity. Damping effectiveness is robust across network positions, suggesting that resilience gains from institutional investment or structural diversification apply broadly.
